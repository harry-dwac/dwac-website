Part I: DWAC Evidence Rules Framework

1.1 General Principles

The Digital World Arbitration Centre (DWAC) has established this comprehensive Evidence Guidance to provide parties with clear standards for preparing, submitting, and presenting evidence in digital dispute proceedings. This framework is designed to balance procedural efficiency with the fundamental requirements of fairness and due process.

The principles governing evidence in DWAC proceedings are grounded in international arbitration best practices, including the IBA Rules on the Taking of Evidence in International Arbitration, while incorporating specific adaptations for digital-native disputes. Parties shall submit evidence that is authentic, relevant, and material to the issues in dispute.

1.2 Burden of Proof

The burden of establishing facts lies upon the party alleging them. In digital disputes, this principle applies with particular relevance to:

  • Ownership and control of digital assets
  • Transaction authenticity and integrity
  • Smart contract execution and performance
  • AI system behavior and decision-making processes
  • Data breaches and security incidents

1.3 Standard of Proof

The arbitral tribunal shall determine the standard of proof applicable to specific issues, taking into account the nature of the dispute and the parties' reasonable expectations. In general, facts are established on a balance of probabilities, though the tribunal may apply a higher standard for serious allegations or complex technical matters.

1.4 Admissibility of Evidence

Evidence shall be admissible if it is:

  • Relevant to a material issue in the proceedings
  • Authentic and capable of verification
  • Obtained through lawful means
  • Not subject to applicable privileges

1.5 Digital-Native Evidence Principles

Recognizing the unique nature of digital disputes, DWAC has established the following principles for digital-native evidence:

Principle of Native Format: Digital evidence shall be submitted in its original electronic format whenever possible, preserving metadata, timestamps, and system-generated attributes.

Principle of Integrity Verification: Parties shall demonstrate that digital evidence has not been altered since its creation or collection, using industry-standard hash verification or blockchain anchoring where appropriate.

Principle of Technical Context: Digital evidence shall be accompanied by sufficient technical context to enable the tribunal to understand its significance, including system documentation, data dictionaries, and expert explanations where necessary.

Part II: Electronic Evidence Standards

2.1 Definition and Categories

Electronic evidence encompasses all information stored, processed, or transmitted in digital form that is offered as evidence in proceedings. DWAC recognizes the following primary categories:

Communications Data

Emails, instant messages, chat logs, and other electronic communications

Transaction Records

Blockchain transactions, payment records, and financial data

System Logs

Server logs, access logs, and system event records

Digital Content

Documents, images, videos, and multimedia files

2.2 Authentication Requirements

Electronic evidence must be properly authenticated before it may be considered by the tribunal. Authentication requires demonstration that the evidence is what its proponent claims it to be.

Acceptable Authentication Methods:

  • Direct Testimony: A witness with personal knowledge can testify to the creation, transmission, or receipt of the electronic record
  • Hash Verification: Cryptographic hash values demonstrating that the file has not been altered
  • Digital Signatures: Verification of digital signatures applied to the document
  • Metadata Analysis: Examination of system-generated metadata including timestamps, author information, and modification history
  • Chain of Custody: Documentation of the secure handling and storage of the evidence
  • Expert Testimony: Technical expert analysis of the evidence and its environment

2.3 Best Practice Standards

Email Evidence

  • Submit complete email headers showing routing information
  • Preserve attachments in original format
  • Provide full chain of custody documentation
  • Include metadata printouts showing send/receive timestamps

Database Records

  • Export data in queryable format with data dictionary
  • Provide database schema documentation
  • Include audit trail information
  • Document any data transformation or migration processes

Social Media and Platform Data

  • Use platform-certified data export tools where available
  • Preserve URL and timestamp information
  • Document account ownership and access
  • Include page capture with metadata

2.4 Hearsay Considerations

Electronic records that would traditionally constitute hearsay may be admissible in DWAC proceedings under the following conditions:

  • The record was made in the regular course of business
  • The record was maintained as part of routine data management practices
  • The record was created at or near the time of the events it records
  • The record was created by persons with knowledge of the recorded events
  • Alternative evidence of authenticity is available

Part III: Blockchain Evidence Guidelines

3.1 Overview

Blockchain technology presents unique challenges and opportunities for evidence in digital disputes. The immutable nature of blockchain records, combined with their decentralized storage and cryptographic verification, creates a robust foundation for evidence presentation. DWAC has developed specific guidelines to address the evidentiary implications of blockchain-based transactions and records.

3.2 Blockchain Record Characteristics

Blockchain records possess several characteristics relevant to evidentiary proceedings:

Immutability

Once confirmed, records cannot be altered without network consensus

Transparency

Public blockchains allow independent verification of transaction history

Timestamping

Transactions include verifiable timestamps from block confirmation

3.3 Presenting Blockchain Evidence

Parties presenting blockchain-based evidence shall provide:

  • Transaction Hash: The unique identifier for the specific transaction
  • Block Number: The block in which the transaction was confirmed
  • Block Explorer Verification: Verification from an independent blockchain explorer showing transaction details
  • Smart Contract Code: If applicable, the source code of any smart contract involved
  • Wallet Addresses: The blockchain addresses of the parties involved
  • Network Documentation: Description of the blockchain network and its consensus mechanism

3.4 Smart Contract Evidence

Disputes involving smart contracts require specific evidence considerations:

For Smart Contract Disputes, Parties Should Submit:

  • The complete source code of the smart contract
  • Documentation of the deployment transaction and deployed bytecode
  • Transaction history showing contract interactions
  • Gas consumption and state changes documentation
  • Expert analysis of contract execution and intended behavior

3.5 Oracle and External Data Evidence

Where smart contracts rely on external data sources (oracles), parties should present:

  • Documentation of the oracle service or mechanism used
  • Records of data requests and responses
  • Evidence of data source reliability and accuracy
  • Chain of custody for externally-sourced data

3.6 Cross-Chain and Layer-2 Evidence

For disputes involving cross-chain transactions or Layer-2 solutions, parties shall provide comprehensive documentation of the entire transaction path, including bridge records, proof generation, and verification mechanisms.

Part IV: AI-Generated Content Evidence

4.1 Introduction

The increasing prevalence of artificial intelligence systems in commercial and personal activities has created novel evidentiary challenges. AI-generated content may serve as evidence in DWAC proceedings, whether as the subject of dispute (e.g., IP ownership questions) or as evidence of system behavior or communications. This section establishes guidelines for the admission and evaluation of AI-generated content.

4.2 Categories of AI-Generated Evidence

AI Communications

Chatbot interactions, AI assistant conversations, automated customer service records

AI-Generated Media

AI-created images, videos, audio, and text content

AI Decision Records

Machine learning model outputs, algorithmic decisions, automated processing logs

AI Training Data

Training datasets, model weights, and related documentation

4.3 Authentication of AI-Generated Content

Parties submitting AI-generated content must address the following authentication requirements:

Authentication Checklist:

  • 1 Source Identification: Document the AI system or service that generated the content, including version information and configuration details
  • 2 Generation Context: Provide the input data, prompts, or parameters used to generate the content
  • 3 Timestamp Verification: Demonstrate when the content was generated and any relevant processing timestamps
  • 4 Chain of Integrity: Show that the content has not been altered since generation
  • 5 Reproducibility: Where possible, demonstrate that similar inputs produce consistent outputs

4.4 AI System Behavior Evidence

Disputes may require evidence of how an AI system behaved, decided, or processed information. For such evidence, parties should provide:

  • System Logs: Complete logs of system operations, API calls, and processing steps
  • Decision Records: Documentation of algorithmic decision-making processes, including input data and output results
  • Model Documentation: Technical documentation describing the AI model's architecture, training data, and intended behavior
  • Expert Analysis: Technical expert testimony on AI system behavior and potential biases
  • Comparative Testing: Results of testing the AI system with similar inputs

4.5 Chain of Custody for AI Evidence

Maintaining a proper chain of custody is essential for AI-generated evidence. Parties shall document:

  • All parties who accessed or processed the AI-generated content
  • All systems used to generate, store, or process the content
  • All transformations or modifications made to the content
  • Security measures in place during evidence preservation
  • Any AI tools used in the evidence gathering or analysis process

4.6 Weight and Evaluation

The tribunal shall consider the following factors when evaluating AI-generated evidence:

  • The reliability and transparency of the AI system that generated the content
  • The availability and completeness of documentation
  • The potential for AI hallucinations, biases, or errors
  • The complexity of the AI system and understanding of its behavior
  • Industry standards for similar AI applications
  • Expert testimony on AI system reliability

Important Note: AI-generated content may be subject to greater scrutiny regarding accuracy and reliability. The tribunal may require corroborating evidence or expert testimony to establish the weight to be given to AI-generated content.

Part V: Evidence Submission Procedures

5.1 General Submission Requirements

All evidence submitted to DWAC proceedings shall comply with the following requirements:

Document Format Requirements

  • PDF format preferred for documents; other formats accepted with justification
  • All documents must be legible and complete
  • Provide both original format and PDF transcription where applicable
  • File naming should clearly identify content and language

5.2 Evidence List Requirements

Each party shall submit a comprehensive list of evidence (Documentary Evidence List) identifying all evidence to be presented, including:

  • Sequential exhibit number
  • Brief description of document or item
  • Number of pages or items
  • Whether original or copy
  • Language of document
  • Relevance to disputed issues

5.3 Submission Timeline

Phase Standard Timeline Simplified Procedure
Statement of Claim/Defense With submission With submission
Documentary Evidence List 30 days after PO1 15 days after PO1
Document Production Requests 30 days after Evidence List Waived
Evidence Submission Deadline 60 days before hearing 15 days before hearing
Expert Reports 45 days before hearing 10 days before hearing

5.4 Document Production Procedures

Requests for document production shall comply with the following requirements:

Request Requirements:

  • Identify specific documents or categories with precision
  • Explain relevance to disputed issues
  • Demonstrate that requested documents are in the other party's possession, custody, or control
  • Describe timeframe for requested documents
  • Identify any confidentiality concerns

5.5 Expert Evidence Procedures

Parties intending to rely on expert evidence shall submit:

  • Expert's Declaration: Statement of independence and qualifications
  • Terms of Reference: Specific questions or issues the expert will address
  • Expert Report: Comprehensive report including methodology, analysis, and conclusions
  • Supporting Materials: All data, documents, and references relied upon

Expert Report Requirements:

  • Expert's qualifications and relevant experience
  • Instructions received and any limitations
  • Summary of methodology used
  • Facts and assumptions relied upon
  • Analysis and reasoning
  • Conclusions and opinions
  • Statement confirming independence

5.6 Language and Translation

Evidence in languages other than the proceedings language shall be accompanied by certified translations. The tribunal may require full or summary translations as appropriate to the significance of the document.

5.7 Confidentiality

Parties may designate certain evidence as confidential. Such designations shall:

  • Identify the specific confidential information
  • Provide justification for the confidential designation
  • Propose appropriate protective measures

The tribunal will determine appropriate procedures for handling confidential evidence, which may include in camera review, redactions, or protective orders.
