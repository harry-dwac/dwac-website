Illustrative Cases

Learn how DWAC resolves digital world disputes through these representative case studies

Note: These are illustrative cases based on common digital world disputes. All names, amounts, and specific details are fictional and for educational purposes only.
1

Cross-border Digital Platform Account Dispute

Dispute Background

A user from Country A had their account suspended by a digital platform headquartered in Country B, allegedly for violating platform policies. The user claimed the suspension was wrongful and sought reinstatement plus compensation for lost business revenue totaling $50,000.

Key Issues

  • Whether the platform's suspension decision was procedurally fair
  • Whether the user's due process rights were violated
  • Applicable law: User's location vs. Platform's terms of service
  • Evidence: Digital logs, communications, and account activity

Arbitration Process

  • Arbitrator: Single arbitrator with expertise in digital platform governance
  • Procedural Language: English
  • Hearing: Virtual hearing conducted via secure video platform
  • Evidence: Electronic documents, server logs, and testimony

Outcome

The arbitrator found that:

  • The platform failed to provide adequate notice and opportunity to respond
  • The suspension was partially justified but procedurally defective
  • Award: Account reinstated; $15,000 compensation for lost revenue; no punitive damages
Key Takeaway: Digital platforms must ensure procedural fairness even in policy enforcement actions.
2

Smart Contract Performance Dispute

Dispute Background

Two parties entered into a smart contract for the delivery of digital services worth 2 ETH (approximately $4,000 at the time). The contract automatically released payment upon delivery confirmation. Party A claimed services were not delivered as specified; Party B argued the automated confirmation proved performance.

Key Issues

  • Interpretation of "satisfactory delivery" in smart contract code
  • Whether automated confirmation is conclusive evidence
  • Gap between code execution and contractual intent
  • Applicable standards for digital service quality

Arbitration Process

  • Arbitrators: Three arbitrators (tech expert, legal expert, industry practitioner)
  • Procedural Language: English
  • Evidence: Smart contract code, on-chain transactions, communications, expert testimony

Outcome

The tribunal found that:

  • The smart contract code was ambiguous on quality standards
  • Automated confirmation was not conclusive evidence of satisfactory performance
  • Award: Partial refund of 0.8 ETH; code modification recommendations for future contracts
Key Takeaway: Smart contracts require clear specification of performance standards; automation does not eliminate the need for dispute resolution mechanisms.
3

Virtual Asset Ownership Dispute

Dispute Background

A dispute arose over ownership of a valuable virtual asset (in-game item) worth approximately $12,000. Party A claimed to have legitimately purchased the asset; Party B claimed the asset was stolen from their account and subsequently sold.

Key Issues

  • Determining rightful ownership of virtual assets
  • Good faith purchaser vs. original owner rights
  • Platform's role in asset transfer and verification
  • Applicable law: Virtual world rules vs. real-world property law

Arbitration Process

  • Arbitrator: Single arbitrator with gaming industry expertise
  • Procedural Language: Chinese
  • Evidence: Transaction logs, account history, platform records, communications

Outcome

The arbitrator found that:

  • The asset was indeed stolen from Party B's account
  • Party A was a good faith purchaser but not the rightful owner
  • The platform had inadequate security measures
  • Award: Asset returned to Party B; Party A compensated $3,000 by platform; platform to improve security
Key Takeaway: Virtual assets can be subject to real-world property principles; platforms bear responsibility for security.
4

AI-Generated Content Copyright Dispute

Dispute Background

A company used AI to generate marketing content that closely resembled a competitor's branded materials. The competitor claimed copyright infringement and sought damages of $100,000 plus injunction.

Key Issues

  • Copyright ownership of AI-generated content
  • Standard for "substantial similarity" in AI outputs
  • Responsibility: AI user vs. AI developer vs. AI itself
  • Appropriate remedies

Arbitration Process

  • Arbitrators: Three arbitrators (IP law expert, AI technology expert, industry representative)
  • Procedural Language: English
  • Evidence: AI training data, output comparison, expert analysis, damages calculation

Outcome

The tribunal found that:

  • AI-generated content can infringe existing copyrights
  • The company failed to implement adequate safeguards
  • The AI developer was not liable (terms of service disclaimer valid)
  • Award: $25,000 damages; injunction against further use; company to implement AI content review process
Key Takeaway: Users of AI tools bear responsibility for outputs; due diligence required even with automated generation.
5

Cross-border E-commerce Transaction Dispute

Dispute Background

A buyer in Country A ordered custom-made digital products from a seller in Country B, paying $8,000. The seller delivered products that the buyer claimed did not match specifications. The seller argued specifications were ambiguous and delivery was accepted.

Key Issues

  • Contract interpretation: What constituted "acceptable delivery"?
  • Cross-border jurisdiction and applicable law
  • Digital product quality assessment
  • Whether acceptance was final

Arbitration Process

  • Arbitrator: Single arbitrator experienced in cross-border digital commerce
  • Procedural Language: English (with Chinese translation support)
  • Evidence: Contract terms, communications, product samples, expert assessment

Outcome

The arbitrator found that:

  • The specifications were ambiguous; both parties shared responsibility
  • Delivery was partially deficient
  • Award: Seller to revise products within 30 days; buyer to pay additional $1,000 for expanded scope; each party bears own costs
Key Takeaway: Clear specifications are critical in cross-border digital transactions; mediation-style solutions can preserve business relationships.
